The Gale Legacy

In the early 1970s, from an unlikely factory in the heart of Mayfair, Ira Gale assembled a team of visionaries who created some of the most iconic British hi-fi equipment ever made. For over 40 years, Dave Smith supported Gale owners with unmatched dedication. That legacy continues through Vintage Gale, preserving these remarkable speakers for future generations.

The Original Home of Gale:
23 Bruton Place, Mayfair

In the 1970s, Gale Electronics operated from an unlikely factory location in the heart of London.

This was where Ira Gale, Jon Bannenberg, Billy Woodman and David Lyth created the legendary GS401 loudspeaker.

The Mayfair address became synonymous with high-end British audio, a testament to the company's uncompromising approach to engineering and design. Today, Bruton Place remains a pilgrimage site for Gale enthusiasts.

Ira Gale:
The Legacy of Gale Electronics

Ira Gale's vision combined avant-garde design, musical training, and uncompromising engineering.

The GS401 and GT2101 turntable became icons of British hi-fi, representing a philosophy that sound quality was paramount, regardless of cost or convention.

As founder and driving force of Gale Electronics, Ira assembled a team of brilliant minds and gave them the freedom to pursue perfection. His legacy lives on in every restored GS401.

Jon Bannenberg:
The Vision Behind the 401

Bannenberg's industrial design turned the GS401 into a sculptural object as much as a loudspeaker.

Best known for his yacht designs for celebrities and royalty, Bannenberg brought the same uncompromising aesthetic to the GS401. The chrome-capped design was brutal, beautiful, and entirely functional.

His work proved that industrial design and acoustic engineering could coexist without compromise. The GS401's appearance is as iconic today as it was in the 1970s.

Billy Woodman:
The Engineering Mind

Billy Woodman refined the GS401's performance before founding ATC, now a world leader in professional loudspeakers.

Woodman's engineering expertise was critical to the GS401's success. He understood that a sealed-box design, properly executed, could deliver bass performance that rivaled much larger ported designs.

After Gale, he went on to establish Acoustic Transducer Company (ATC), creating some of the most respected studio monitors in the world. His influence on British audio engineering is immeasurable.

David Lyth:
Engineering the Sound of Gale

David Lyth built the soundproof chamber used to test every GS401 against reference standards.

His meticulous approach to acoustic measurement ensured that every pair of GS401s met exacting performance criteria. No speaker left Bruton Place without Lyth's approval.

He later founded Volt Loudspeakers, continuing to shape modern British audio with the same rigor and attention to detail that defined his work at Gale. His legacy is one of precision and uncompromising standards.
